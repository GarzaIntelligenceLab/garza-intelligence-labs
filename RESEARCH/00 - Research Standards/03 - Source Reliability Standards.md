# 03 - Source Reliability Standards

> **Status:** Approved
>
> **Version:** 1.0
>
> **Last Updated:** July 2026

---

# Purpose

Define the standards Garza Intelligence Labs uses to evaluate, verify, and trust information.

Every piece of research conducted by Garza Intelligence Labs must be supported by reliable evidence.

The objective is not to collect information.

The objective is to build operational intelligence that can be trusted.

Every recommendation, product decision, and engineering decision is only as reliable as the information supporting it.

---

# Philosophy

Not all information is equal.

Some information is factual.

Some information is opinion.

Some information is observation.

Some information is speculation.

The responsibility of Garza Intelligence Labs is to understand the difference.

Research should never confuse confidence with certainty.

---

# Mission

Verify before believing.

Corroborate before concluding.

Question before accepting.

Document before recommending.

Every claim should strengthen the overall integrity of the Garza Intelligence Labs Knowledge Base.

---

# Core Principles

Every source should be:

Credible.

Relevant.

Current.

Transparent.

Repeatable.

Verifiable.

Objective whenever possible.

---

# Source Hierarchy

Every source should be classified according to its level of reliability.

## Tier One — Primary Sources

Highest Reliability

Examples include:

Official documentation.

Official Disney announcements.

Government publications.

Academic journals.

Original research.

Direct interviews.

Internal operational data.

Whenever available, primary sources should always be preferred.

---

## Tier Two — Professional Sources

High Reliability

Examples include:

Industry reports.

Recognized subject matter experts.

Professional publications.

Established research organizations.

Verified operational databases.

These sources provide valuable interpretation while remaining evidence-driven.

---

## Tier Three — Community Intelligence

Moderate Reliability

Examples include:

Experienced community members.

Discussion forums.

Guest trip reports.

Long-term enthusiasts.

Public datasets.

Community intelligence often identifies operational patterns before official documentation.

These findings should always be independently verified whenever possible.

---

## Tier Four — Informational Sources

Supporting Reliability

Examples include:

Blogs.

News articles.

YouTube videos.

Podcasts.

Social media.

These sources may provide useful context but should rarely serve as the sole basis for important conclusions.

---

## Tier Five — Speculation

Lowest Reliability

Examples include:

Rumors.

Leaks.

Anonymous posts.

Unverified claims.

Predictions.

Speculative content should never be presented as established fact.

If retained, it should always be clearly labeled.

---

# Verification Standards

Whenever possible, important conclusions should be supported by multiple independent sources.

Research should prioritize:

Primary confirmation.

Independent confirmation.

Historical consistency.

Operational consistency.

The greater the impact of a decision, the greater the required confidence.

---

# Conflicting Information

Conflicting information should never be ignored.

Instead, researchers should:

Document the disagreement.

Identify the source of the conflict.

Evaluate the credibility of each source.

Record the current confidence level.

Update conclusions as stronger evidence becomes available.

Uncertainty is valuable information.

---

# Historical Preservation

Information should never be deleted simply because it becomes outdated.

Instead:

Archive previous information.

Document why changes occurred.

Preserve historical context.

Track the evolution of knowledge over time.

Historical intelligence often explains present-day behavior.

---

# Citation Standards

Every meaningful conclusion should include:

Original source.

Publication date.

Date accessed.

Researcher.

Confidence level.

Supporting evidence.

Future researchers should always be able to understand how a conclusion was reached.

---

# Confidence Levels

Every research conclusion should receive a confidence rating.

## Confirmed

Supported by multiple high-quality sources.

Operationally reliable.

---

## High Confidence

Strong evidence exists.

Minor uncertainty remains.

---

## Moderate Confidence

Evidence suggests accuracy.

Additional verification recommended.

---

## Low Confidence

Limited supporting evidence.

Treat cautiously.

---

## Unknown

Insufficient evidence exists.

Additional research required.

Unknown should never be mistaken for incorrect.

---

# Bias Awareness

Every source contains potential bias.

Researchers should evaluate:

Financial incentives.

Institutional interests.

Personal opinions.

Selection bias.

Historical context.

Recognizing bias strengthens research.

Ignoring bias weakens it.

---

# Continuous Validation

Research should never become permanent truth.

As new information becomes available:

Re-evaluate conclusions.

Increase or decrease confidence.

Preserve historical versions.

Update operational recommendations.

Knowledge should continuously evolve.

---

# Research Integrity

Garza Intelligence Labs should never:

Manipulate evidence.

Ignore contradictory findings.

Overstate certainty.

Present speculation as fact.

Favor conclusions over evidence.

Integrity is more valuable than being correct quickly.

---

# Success Criteria

The Source Reliability Standards succeed when:

Research remains trustworthy.

Recommendations are supported by evidence.

Future researchers understand why conclusions were reached.

Confidence increases as knowledge grows.

The Knowledge Base becomes stronger with every contribution.

---

# Final Principle

Information is abundant.

Trusted knowledge is rare.

Garza Intelligence Labs exists to transform information into operational intelligence through disciplined research, transparent reasoning, and continuous verification.

Every source contributes to understanding.

Every conclusion earns its confidence.
